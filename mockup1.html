Democrats attack Obama over imminent Tehran deal

Matt Spence and Alexandra Frean in Washington
Published at 12:01AM, March 28 2015

Barack Obama has come under fire from within his party for giving in to Iranian demands over a nuclear deal that is expected as soon as tomorrow.

While John Kerry and Mohammad Javad Zarif, the Iranian foreign minister, continued negotiations last night in Lausanne, Switzerland, politicians at home warned the president that they would do all they could to scupper an agreement.

Robert Menendez, the leading Democrat on the Senate foreign relations committee, accused the president of wanting “a deal for a deal’s sake”, saying that Mr Obama was not simply “inching closer to Iran’s negotiating position but leaping toward it with both feet”.

The British foreign secretary was in Washington urging those in Congress not to undermine the negotiations as the end of March deadline approaches.

Philip Hammond met yesterday with Senator Bob Corker, the Republican chairman of the foreign relations committee, to allay concerns that the United States is giving too much ground in its attempts to dissuade Iran from developing a nuclear weapon in exchange for relief from international sanctions.

Mr Hammond told journalists that he wanted to stop attempts in Congress to enforce tougher sanctions on Iran.

“We certainly would want Congress’s engagement in this to be of a different nature, not simply marking up a sanctions bill,” he said.

His comments came after a report by Associated Press that American negotiators were prepared to allow Iran to run hundreds of centrifuges at a fortified bunker previously used for uranium enrichment.

As part of the reported agreement, centrifuges at the formerly secret Fordo facility would be allowed to run non-nuclear elements such as zinc, xenon and germanium for use in medicine, industry or science.

In exchange, Iran would agree to reduce the number of centrifuges at other facilities, adhere to strict inspections at Fordo and accept other restrictions on its nuclear-related activities. Last week, American officials said that the two sides were close to agreement on a cap of 6,000 centrifuges at Iran’s primary enrichment site, Natanz, more than triple the original American negotiation position.

Foreign ministers from Russia, China, Britain, France and Germany are scheduled to join Mr Kerry and Mr Zarif in Switzerland this weekend. Mr Hammond said that there was some leeway for pushing back the Tuesday deadline to the middle of April, but this may not satisfy US congressional figures on either side. This month, 47 Republican senators sent a controversial letter to Iranian leaders warning that any settlement reached would not outlast Mr Obama’s presidency.

One of the signatories, Marco Rubio, the Republican presidential hopeful, said that he would “absolutely” be willing to revoke any deal signed by Mr Obama that allowed Iran to retain enrichment capabilities.

“I just think the deal is premised on an agreement on something that is totally unacceptable, and quite frankly, abandons almost a decade of sanctions,” Mr Rubio said.

This week, the Republican congressmen Peter Roskam and Lee Zeldin called on colleagues to choke off funding for American participation in the nuclear negotiations, warning that the deal that was emerging would “utterly fail to dismantle Iran’s nuclear programme.”
